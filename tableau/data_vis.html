iShowAccessibility

Digital technology is often assumed to be universally accessible, yet real-world data showcases that many websites unintentionally exclude users with disabilities. Utilizing over 3,500 documented accessibility violations across 448 websites spanning domains including health, education, government, news, technology, and e-commerce, our team analyzed where accessibility barriers occur, what forms they take, and how severe their impact is. Our findings create clear actionable insight to help organizations understand where technology fails and how it can better serve everyone.

Accessibility Violations by Domain

Understanding how accessibility barriers differ across industries is essential to identifying where digital exclusion is most prevalent. Clear patterns of systemic inequity in web design are obvious when analyzing the number of accessibility violations within each domain category including health, education, government, and e-commerce. Not all industries will prioritize accessibility equally. Certain domains consistently demonstrate higher concentrations of violations, indicating structural design practices that unintentionally exclude users with disabilities. These disparities showcase where accessibility risks are most severe and where remediation efforts could produce the greatest improvement in digital accessibility. The visualization provides a clear data-driven perspective on which sectors place users most at risk of exclusion and where targeted accessibility interventions would yield the highest impact.

Most Common Accessibility Violation Types

Beyond identifying which industries experience the most violations, it is equally important to understand the kinds of accessibility failures that occur frequently and whether patterns emerge across syntactic, semantic, and layout-related issues. By examining the most common violation types across domains, we find that a small subset of recurring accessibility errors accounts for a disproportionate share of total barriers. This concentration suggests that meaningful improvements in accessibility could be achieved by prioritizing fixes for only a handful of high-frequency issues, including missing alternative text, insufficient color contrast, or improper heading structures. Recognizing these recurring barriers transforms accessibility from an abstract challenge into an actionable design strategy. Enabling organizations to focus resources on the changes would most effectively expand inclusivity access across thousands of webpages.

Severe Pages/Categories

While frequency of violations is important, the severity of those violations ultimately determines their real-world impact on users. Some webpages may contain numerous minor issues, whereas others exhibit moderate to serious accessibility failures that significantly affect navigation, comprehension, or interaction for individuals with disabilities. By identifying the pages and domains with the most severe accessibility issues, patterns are uncovered in how different industries unintentionally exclude users. These patterns reveal hidden design assumptions, including reliance on visually complex layouts, insufficient semantic structure, or inaccessible interactive components that contribute disproportionately to harmful user experiences. The analysis shows that many violations fall within moderate or serious severity levels, confirming that accessibility challenges are not just technical mistakes but barriers to equitable interaction. Moreover, a relatively small group of pages contributes heavily to overall accessibility harm, indicating that targeted remediation could substantially reduce exclusion.

Proposed Solutions

Based on the analysis of the datasets, accessibility failures are systemic yet highly concentrated. This means meaningful improvements can be achieved through targeted, data-driven interventions rather than broad, unfocused changes.

  1. Prioritize High-Impact Violation Types: A few recurring WCAG violations, including missing alt text, poor color contrast, and improper semantic structure, cause most accessibility barriers, so fixing them quickly improves usability for screen reader, low-vision, and keyboard-only users.
    • Fix the most common and high-impact violation types first to achieve the greatest improvement with limited resources.
    • Rank and remediate pages with the highest severity or overall accessibility impact.
    • Focus on core user journeys such as forms, checkout, login, and navigation.
  2. Conduct Domain-Specific Accessibility Audits: Organizations should perform industry-tailored accessibility audits, as sector-specific design practices and regulations require targeted reviews to achieve real reductions in exclusion rather than just surface-level compliance.
    • Identify common violation patterns within their domain.
    • Benchmark accessibility performance against similar organizations within their domain.
    • Allocate resources to highest-risk interaction patterns.
  3. Embedded Accessibility into Development and Culture: Sustainable inclusion requires integrating accessibility into development workflows and organizational priorities, making it a proactive foundation rather than a reactive obligation.
    • Embedding automated WCAG testing into CI/CD pipelines to detect issues before release.
    • Preventing regressions in accessible components through continuous monitoring.
    • Shifting organizational mindset from regulatory compliance to inclusive design that benefits all users.

Together, these proposals demonstrate that digital exclusion is solvable. Through targeted improvement, domain-specific insight, and systemic integration, organizations can move beyond compliance towards true digital equity and inclusive user experience.
